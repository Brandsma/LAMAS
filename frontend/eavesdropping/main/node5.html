Perfect Communication with Eavesdropping

While the perfect communication may be impervious to naive, incidental eavesdropping, a sufficiently clever agent, let's say one named Eve, could potentially mediate the entire exchange of information, passing off her own public key as those of the expected agent, decrypting and re-encrypting all passing information. For a better understanding of this circumstance, let us consider the below sequence in conjunction with Fig. .

  1. A sends Public Key A to B
  2. E intercepts Public Key A
  3. E sends Public Key E to B
  4. B sends Public Key B to A
  5. E intercepts Public Key B
  6. E sends Public Key E to A
  7. A encrypts mA with PubE and sends it to B
  8. E intercepts encrypted mA,
  9. E decrypts mA and re-encrypts it with Public Key B
  10. E sends encrypted mA to B
  11. B encrypts MB with PubE and sends it to A
  12. E intercepts encrypted mB,
  13. E decrypts mB and re-encrypts it with Public Key A
  14. E sends encrypted mB to A
  15. A decrypts enmB with Private Key A
  16. B decrypts enmA with Private Key B

Figure: Perfect communication with Eavesdropping Kripke Structure

Qualitatively, it should be understood that when Bob thinks he is receiving Alice's public key, he is actually receiving Eve's, so when he encrypts his message for Alice, he actually encrypts it for Eve, who can then decrypt it and re-encrypt it with what Alice will think is Bob's public key, but is also actually Eve. In essence, all communication is moderated though Eve. Alice and Bob never directly interact.
