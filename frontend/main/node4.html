Perfect Communication

Aquainting ourselves with the pertinent mechanisms of the perfect communication configuration, we refer to Fig. and can understand the depicted actions in the following order:

  1. A sends Public Key A to B
  2. B sends Public Key B to A
  3. A encrypts mA with pubB and sends it to B
  4. B encrypts mB with pubA and sends it to A
  5. A decrypts enMB with Private Key A
  6. B decrypts enMA with Private Key B

Figure: Perfect communication diagram.

What is important to understand is that both agents send their public keys to each other. These public keys are specifically associated to their own private keys, which they do not share. Thus messages can be encrypted with the opposite agent's public key such that these messages may only be decrypted by that agent's private key once they have received the message. For example, Alice encrypts a message with Bob's public key . When Bob receives the message, he decrypts it with his private key , which only he has. In this way, the message should be only readable by Bob. The intention here is to maintain private communication though encryption. Please refer to Encryption [link] section for greater detail. Also, note that because perfect communication is assumed (free of deletion errors), no confirmation-of-knowledge messages are necessary. However, the subsequent formalism could well be constructed atop a confirmation protocol for imperfect communication.
